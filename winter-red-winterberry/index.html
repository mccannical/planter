    Winter Red Winterberry — Complete Care Guide¶

    Ilex verticillata 'Winter Red'¶

    Home | Layout | Construction | Build Guide | Materials | Soil | Drainage | Checklist | Winterberry | Azalea | Bluebells | Phlox

    Zone: 7b–8a | Hardiness: USDA 3–9 | Type: Deciduous shrub Mature size: 6–8' tall x 6–8' wide (may stay smaller in raised bed: 4–6')

    At a Glance¶

    Attribute Value
    Sun Full sun (6+ hrs) to part shade (2–6 hrs)
    Water Medium to wet — native wetland shrub
    Soil pH 3.5–6.0 (strongly acidic)
    Fertilizer Holly-Tone or acid-specific, 2x/year
    Bloom May–June (small white flowers)
    Berry display October–February (brilliant red on bare stems)
    Pollinator required Yes — 'Southern Gentleman' male within 50 ft
    Deciduous Yes — drops leaves in fall, berries persist on bare stems

    Sun Needs¶

    Full sun (6+ hours) to part shade (2–6 hours).

    Minimum 4 hours direct sun for any berry production. More sun = more berries, but also higher water demand.

    Proven Winners: "We recommend a minimum of four hours of sun each day for the best display, since full sun encourages abundant flower production, pollination, and fruit production."

    Proven Winners: "In very dark conditions, flowering and fruiting can be significantly diminished, or may not occur at all."

    Raised bed on concrete: The patio will likely be sunny — this works in favor of berry production. However, concrete radiates heat and reflects light, so monitor soil moisture closely in summer.

    Water Needs¶

    Preferred range: Medium to wet. This is a native wetland shrub that tolerates boggy conditions.

    Virginia Tech: "Moisture: Wet to moist but will do fine in soils with 'average' moisture regimes."

    Proven Winners: "If your winterberry hollies experience drought stress, they may not form fruit or may drop any fruit that was beginning to form."

    Virginia Tech: "Fruit may abort during periods of drought."

    Seasonal Watering Schedule¶

    Season Frequency Depth Notes
    Spring (Mar–May) 2x/week Deep to 6" Increase as temps rise
    Summer (Jun–Aug) 2–3x/week Deep until draining Drought = fruit abortion. Mulch essential.
    Fall (Sep–Nov) 1–2x/week Moderate Reduce as temps cool
    Winter (Dec–Feb) Every 2–3 weeks if no rain Light Raised bed dries faster; don't let roots freeze-dry

    Signs of Trouble¶

    • Over-watering: Root rot (Phytophthora) — yellowing, dieback. Uncommon for this species given wetland origins.
    • Under-watering: Fruit abortion, leaf scorch, premature fruit drop, chlorosis.

    Soil Needs¶

    pH 3.5–6.0. Do not exceed 6.5 under any circumstances.

    NC State Extension: "Neutral to alkaline soils may cause chlorosis (yellowing of leaves) and even death."

    Missouri Botanical Garden: "Plants do poorly in neutral to alkaline soils where they are susceptible to chlorosis and often die."

    • Adaptable to sandy, loam, or clay
    • Prefers moist, acidic, organic loams
    • High organic matter content preferred

    Concrete pH Drift Warning¶

    Concrete leaches lime and raises pH over time — direct threat in a bed on concrete.

    Countermeasures: - Test pH every spring with a reliable meter - Apply sulfur granules or acidifying fertilizer each spring - Use rainwater if possible (tap water often pH 7.0–8.5) - Pine needle mulch (pH ~3.5–4.5 as it decomposes) is your friend - Avoid any soil with dolomitic limestone or wood ash amendments

    Nutrient Needs¶

    Low-maintenance native shrub. Over-fertilizing causes more harm than under-fertilizing.

    Fertilizer Type¶

    • Slow-release, acid-specific. Holly-Tone by Espoma (4-3-4 with sulfur) is the industry standard.
    • NPK ratio: 3:1:1 or 3:1:2 (Clemson Extension)
    • Organic preferred — Holly-Tone, cottonseed meal, fish emulsion
    • Avoid synthetic fast-release nitrogen (burns roots in raised beds)

    Schedule¶

    When Action
    Late March–early April Primary feeding as buds swell
    Late August–early September Optional second light feeding
    July–August No fertilizing (peak heat)
    After late September No fertilizing (risk of late-season soft growth)
    Year 1 Minimal to no fertilization — focus on establishment

    Clemson Extension: "Time the fertilizer applications from spring to early summer and in late summer to early fall when it coincides with plant demand."

    Pollinator Requirement — CRITICAL¶

    A male plant is absolutely required for berries. 'Winter Red' is a female-only clone. Winterberry is dioecious — separate male and female plants.

    Required Male: 'Southern Gentleman'¶

    Clemson Extension: "'Southern Gentleman' — Male pollinator with late-season bloom period. Can pollinate 'Winter Red' and 'Winter Gold.'"

    UMD Extension: "Match a late-blooming 'Southern Gentleman' with a late-blooming female such as 'Winter Red.'"

    Pollination Details¶

    • 'Winter Red' is a late-blooming female — male must also be late-blooming
    • DO NOT use 'Jim Dandy' (early bloomer — bloom times won't overlap)
    • Proximity: within ~50 feet
    • Ratio: 1 male pollinates up to 5–20 females depending on distance
    • Male can be in a separate container, different raised bed, or in-ground nearby

    Care & Watering Cycle (Month by Month)¶

    Month Activity
    Jan–Feb Dormant; berries persisting; birds feeding. Light watering if dry in raised bed.
    March Buds swell. Apply Holly-Tone. Light pruning window (only if needed). Test soil pH.
    April Full leaf emergence. Monitor for leafminers. Apply 2–3" acidic mulch. Increase watering.
    May Small white flowers appear. Critical pollination window — avoid pesticides this month. Keep bees happy.
    June Green berries forming. Keep soil consistently moist. Apply second mulch top-dress if needed.
    Jul–Aug Green berries swelling. Drought = fruit drop. Water deeply 2–3x/week. No fertilizer. Check for mites.
    September Berries begin turning bright red. Optional second light fertilizer (early Sept only).
    October Leaves dropping; brilliant red berries exposed on bare stems. Peak ornamental display.
    Nov–Dec Berries persist. Cut stems for holiday arrangements. No maintenance needed.

    Pruning¶

    • Skip pruning most years. Proven Winners recommends not pruning at all.
    • If shaping needed: February–March before bud break
    • Never prune after June (berries developing)
    • Berries form on old wood — spring pruning removes next season's flower buds
    • Cut stems for indoor arrangements (winter) — this IS the ideal "pruning"

    Troubleshooting Guide¶

    No Berries / Sparse Berries¶

    Cause Fix
    No male pollinator nearby Plant 'Southern Gentleman' within 50 ft
    Wrong male cultivar Ensure male is late-blooming (NOT 'Jim Dandy')
    Plant too young (<2–3 years) Wait — may not produce in year 1
    Insufficient sun (<4 hours) Move to sunnier position
    Drought stress in summer Water deeply 2–3x/week; add mulch
    Spring pruning removed buds Avoid pruning; cut only dead/damaged wood

    Yellowing Leaves (Chlorosis)¶

    Most likely cause in a raised bed: pH too high.

    Missouri Botanical Garden: "Plants do poorly in neutral to alkaline soils where they are susceptible to chlorosis and often die."

    Fix: Test pH immediately. Apply sulfur or acidifying fertilizer. Switch to rainwater. Chelated iron foliar spray for immediate green-up while correcting pH long-term.

    Secondary cause: Iron deficiency (often pH-induced).

    Leaf Drop¶

    • Normal: Fall defoliation (this is deciduous)
    • Abnormal spring drop: Root rot (check drainage), drought stress, or pest damage

    Branch Dieback¶

    • Primary suspect: Root rot (Phytophthora or black root rot)
    • Look for brown-black streaking from roots up into stem
    • Improve drainage; remove dead branches; no cure for infected tissue

    Suckering in Raised Bed¶

    Winterberry naturally forms thickets via root suckers. In a raised bed, suckers crowd the space and crowd out other plants.

    Missouri Botanical Garden: "Often suckers to form large thickets or colonies."

    Fix: Remove suckers annually at ground level in early spring. This is an ongoing management commitment.

    Pests & Diseases¶

    Diseases¶

    Disease Symptoms Treatment
    Phytophthora Root Rot Yellowing shoot tips, early leaf drop, twig dieback, brown streaking Prevention only — ensure drainage. Biological fungicides (Serenade) preventatively
    Black Root Rot Stunted growth, chlorosis, black root tips No cure. Remove plant; sterilize bed.
    Tar Spot Yellow spots → reddish-brown → black by fall Remove spotted leaves; improve air circulation
    Powdery Mildew White powdery coating on leaves Minor issue. Copper soap fungicide; improve air circulation

    Insects¶

    Pest Symptoms Organic Treatment
    Leafminers Yellowish-brown blotch mines in leaves Handpick infested leaves; spinosad spray (May)
    Southern Red Mite Yellow speckling, bronze discoloration, fine webbing Strong water spray; insecticidal soap; 2% horticultural oil
    Scale Small brown/gray bumps on stems; yellowing, decline Scrape by hand (light); 2% horticultural oil (heavy)
    Spittlebug White spittle masses; leaf distortion Insecticidal soap on adults

    Clemson HGIC: "Avoid the use of spray pesticides unless absolutely necessary. If spraying is required, always spray late in the evening to reduce impact on pollinators."

    Raised Bed Specific Considerations¶

    1. Suckering — expect and manage annually
    2. pH drift from concrete — test and amend every spring
    3. Faster drying — water more frequently than in-ground, especially summer
    4. Faster nutrient leaching — consider 3 feedings/year vs. 2, or use slow-release osmotic fertilizers
    5. Root confinement — plant will grow more slowly, top out smaller (4–6' vs. 8'). Root-prune or replant every 8–10 years.
    6. Concrete heat — mulch is critical. Consider light-colored paint on exterior of bed walls.
    7. Drip irrigation strongly recommended for consistent moisture

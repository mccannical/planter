## The Compatibility Challenge

Your four plants have **conflicting requirements** that must be designed around. The L-shape is actually ideal — it creates two natural zones.

### Compatibility Matrix

| Plant | pH | Sun | Water | Drainage |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Winter Red Winterberry | **3.5–6.0** (acid) | Full sun (6+ hrs) | Moist to wet | Moderate |
| Mandarin Lights Azalea (x2) | **4.5–5.5** (acid) | Morning sun, PM shade | Evenly moist | Good |
| Virginia Bluebells (x2) | **6.5–7.5** (neutral) | Part to full shade | Moist spring, dry dormant | Good |
| Snowflake Carpet Phlox | **6.0–7.0** (neutral) | Full sun (6+ hrs) | Well-drained, moderate | **Excellent (critical)** |

### Key Conflicts

1. **pH:** Winterberry + Azalea need acid (pH 4.5–5.5). Bluebells + Phlox need neutral (pH 6.0–7.5). A 1–2 unit gap.
2. **Sun:** Winterberry + Phlox want full sun. Bluebells want shade. Azalea wants morning sun / PM shade.
3. **Water:** Bluebells need NO water during summer dormancy. Everything else needs consistent summer moisture.
4. **Drainage:** Phlox needs the fastest-draining soil. Winterberry tolerates boggy conditions.

## Recommended Layout

```
                    │           LONG SIDE (60" x 30")              │
                    │                                              │
                    │   ACID ZONE (pH 4.5–5.5)                     │
                    │                                              │
                    │   ┌──────────────────────────────────────┐   │
                    │   │                                      │   │
                    │   │   WINTERBERRY 'Winter Red'            │   │
                    │   │   (center-back, full sun side)        │   │
                    │   │                                      │   │
                    │   │   AZALEA 'Mandarin Lights' x 2       │   │
                    │   │   (flanking winterberry,              │   │
                    │   │    morning sun with some PM shade     │   │
                    │   │    from the winterberry as it grows)  │   │
                    │   │                                      │   │
                    │   └──────────────────────────────────────┘   │
                    │                                              │
                    │   Pine bark mulch throughout                  │
                    │   Soil: pine bark fines + peat + perlite     │
       ┌────────────┤                                              │
       │            └──────────────────────────────────────────────┘
       │ SHORT SIDE
       │ (48" x 24")        ┌──────────┐
       │                    │ BURIED   │
       │  NEUTRAL ZONE      │ pH       │
       │  (pH 6.0–7.0)      │ BARRIER  │
       │                    └──────────┘
       │
       │  VIRGINIA BLUEBELLS x 2
       │  (shaded side of bed,
       │   mark locations for dormancy,
       │   interplant with hostas/ferns)
       │
       │  SNOWFLAKE CARPET PHLOX
       │  (outer edge, cascading over wall,
       │   gritty well-draining sub-zone)
       │
       │  Leaf mulch + compost (no mulch on phlox crown)
       │  Soil: topsoil + grit + perlite
       │
       └───────────────────────────────────────
```

### Why This Layout Works

| Decision | Reason |
|---|---|
| Winterberry in center-back of long leg | Gets the most sun; tallest plant (6–8') anchors the back |
| Azaleas flanking winterberry | Share the same acid soil zone; get morning sun with PM shade from the winterberry as it matures |
| Bluebells in short leg | Potentially shadier side; neutral soil; separated from acid zone |
| Phlox at outer edge of short leg | Full sun position; cascades over the wall; gritty sub-zone with best drainage; near weep holes |
| Acid zone in long leg | Larger volume (25 cu ft) gives more buffering capacity for pH management |
| Neutral zone in short leg | Smaller volume (16 cu ft) is adequate for 2 small perennials + 1 ground cover |
| pH barrier at junction | Prevents acid amendments from migrating to neutral zone |

## pH Zone Engineering

### Physical Barrier

At the junction between the two L-legs, install a **buried vertical barrier**:

- **Material:** 6–12" strips of rigid HDPE root barrier or cedar board
- **Position:** Standing vertically in the soil at the junction point
- **Depth:** Full soil column depth (16–18" after gravel layer)
- **Purpose:** Minimizes lateral pH migration between zones

### Zone-Specific Soil Mixes

**Long leg (Acid Zone — Winterberry + Azalea):**
- 30–40% aged blended compost
- 20–30% pine bark fines (≤1/4")
- 20–25% peat moss or coir
- 15–20% perlite or horticultural grit
- Elemental sulfur per soil test → target pH 4.5–5.5
- Surface mulch: pine bark, pine needles, or oak leaves (2–3")

**Short leg (Neutral Zone — Bluebells + Phlox):**
- 40% quality loamy topsoil
- 25% aged compost
- 20% coarse grit or pea gravel
- 15% coarse perlite
- No acidifying amendments
- Surface: leaf mulch over bluebells, NO mulch on phlox crown

**Phlox sub-zone (at the edge):**
- 40% topsoil
- 30% coarse grit (3/8")
- 20% chunky perlite
- 10% coarse horticultural sand
- Drains within 30–60 seconds

### Annual pH Maintenance

| Zone | Test When | Target | If Too High | If Too Low |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Acid zone | Early spring | 4.5–5.5 | Apply elemental sulfur or iron sulfate | Unlikely — add small amount of dolomitic lime |
| Neutral zone | Early spring | 6.0–7.0 | Unlikely | Add small amount of lime if drifting acid |

**Concrete pH drift:** The patio leaches lime upward. This may actually help the neutral zone stay neutral, but will fight the acid zone. Budget for annual sulfur applications in the acid zone.

## Companion Planting Notes

### Within the Acid Zone (Long Leg)

Winterberry and azaleas share the same requirements — they're excellent companions. Both want:
- Acid soil (pH 4.5–6.0)
- Consistent moisture
- Similar fertilizer (Holly-Tone)

**Additional acid-zone companions if space permits:**
- Blue flag iris (*Iris virginica*) — wet, acidic, full sun
- Native ferns (*Osmunda regalis*) — acidic wet soil
- Heuchera — acid-tolerant foliage interest
- Blueberries — identical pH needs

### Within the Neutral Zone (Short Leg)

**Bluebells go dormant June–February.** Companions must cover the gap:

| Season | What's Visible |
|---|---|
| Feb–April | Bluebells blooming + phlox blooming (overlapping spring show!) |
| May | Phlox being sheared; bluebells fading; hostas expanding |
| Jun–Sep | Phlox mat (green); hostas/ferns covering bluebell spot |
| Oct–Jan | Phlox mat (semi-evergreen green texture); hostas dormant; bed quiet |

**Best dormancy-coverage companions for the bluebell area:**
- Hostas — expand perfectly as bluebells decline
- Hardy ferns — unfurl in late spring
- Shallow-rooted annuals (impatiens, begonias) — plant carefully over dormant crowns

## Pollinator Setup

**Winter Red winterberry requires a male pollinator for berries.**

### 'Southern Gentleman' Placement

- Place in a **separate container** within 50 feet of 'Winter Red'
- 18–24" deep container, minimum 20" diameter
- Same acid soil mix as the raised bed acid zone
- Same watering and fertilizing schedule as 'Winter Red'
- One 'Southern Gentleman' pollinates up to 5–20 females

**Do NOT use 'Jim Dandy'** — it's an early-blooming male. 'Winter Red' is late-blooming. Bloom times must overlap for pollination.

## Seasonal Calendar — All Plants Combined

| Month | Acid Zone (Winterberry + Azalea) | Neutral Zone (Bluebells + Phlox) |
|---|---|---|
| **Jan** | Dormant. Winterberry berries persisting. | Dormant. Phlox semi-evergreen. Mark bluebell locations. |
| **Feb** | Buds swelling. Light watering if dry. | Bluebell shoots emerging. Phlox fertilizer application. |
| **Mar** | Apply Holly-Tone to both. Azalea buds swelling. Test pH. | Bluebells in active growth. Phlox beginning to bloom. Water both. |
| **Apr** | **Azalea peak bloom** (orange). Winterberry leafing out. Water regularly. | **Bluebell peak bloom** (blue) + **Phlox peak bloom** (white). Spectacular spring display! |
| **May** | Winterberry flowering (small white). Post-bloom azalea pruning + fertilizer. | Shear phlox by 1/3–1/2. Bluebells fading — stop watering their zone. |
| **Jun** | Green winterberry berries forming. Azalea in active growth. Monitor lace bugs. | Bluebells fully dormant. Mark locations. Phlox in summer mode. |
| **Jul** | **Deep water 2–3x/week.** No fertilizer. Monitor mites. | Water phlox every 3–5 days. Do NOT water bluebell area. |
| **Aug** | Continue deep watering. Winterberry berries swelling. | Continue phlox watering. Bluebell area dry and dormant. |
| **Sep** | Berries turning red. Optional light azalea fertilizer (iron sulfate). Reduce water. | Reduce phlox watering. Good time to divide bluebells if needed. |
| **Oct** | Winterberry leaves dropping — red berries on bare stems. Apply mulch to azalea. | Apply leaf mulch over bluebell area. Phlox settling into winter mode. |
| **Nov** | Peak winterberry display. One deep watering post-frost if dry. | Quiet. Phlox green mat provides winter texture. |
| **Dec** | Cut winterberry stems for holiday arrangements. | Dormant. |

## Watering Zones — Important for Irrigation Planning

The bed has **three distinct watering regimes** that need separate management:

| Zone | Spring | Summer | Fall | Winter |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Acid zone** (winterberry + azalea) | 2x/week | **2–3x/week** (critical) | 1–2x/week → reduce | Monthly |
| **Bluebell area** | 2–3x/week | **NONE** (dormant!) | None | None |
| **Phlox area** | Every 5–7 days | Every 3–5 days | Every 10–14 days | Every 3–4 weeks |

**Drip irrigation recommendation:** If installing drip lines, zone them so the bluebell area can be shut off independently in summer. Running summer irrigation over dormant bluebell roots will cause rot.

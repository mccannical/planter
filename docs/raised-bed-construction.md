## Bed Specifications

- **Shape:** L-shaped (two adjoining rectangles)
- **Long side:** 60" x 30" x 24" deep
- **Short side:** 48" x 24" x 24" deep
- **Total soil volume:** ~41 cu ft (~1.5 cu yards)
- **Zone:** 7b–8a

---

## Materials — Use Cedar or ACQ Lumber, Not Metal

Metal raised beds on concrete in zone 7b–8a create a **double heat amplification** effect — metal surfaces reach 100–120°F while concrete radiates heat from below. Cedar or modern ACQ pressure-treated lumber insulates significantly better (80–90°F surface temps).

### Material Comparison

| Material | Pros | Cons | Lifespan |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Cedar / Redwood** | Naturally rot-resistant, best insulation, no chemical treatment needed | Highest cost | 10–20 years |
| **ACQ Pressure-Treated** | Budget-friendly, FDA-approved for food production (no arsenic since 2004) | Requires stainless steel fasteners (reacts with galvanized) | 15–20 years |
| **Galvanized Steel** | Very durable, modern look | Surface temps 100–120°F in summer; heat amplified by concrete below | 15–20+ years |
| **Composite / Synthetic** | Excellent rot resistance, good aesthetics | More expensive than cedar in most markets | 20+ years |

> **UF/IFAS Extension:** *"Cedar, redwood, and synthetic wood are naturally rot-resistant. Do not use railroad ties or old pressure-treated lumber purchased prior to January 2004."*

**Recommendation for this project:** Cedar or ACQ pressure-treated wood. Avoid metal in zone 7b–8a on concrete.

## Bottom Construction — Open with Hardware Cloth Basket

**Do NOT** put soil directly on concrete (washes out, concrete alkalinity affects pH).
**Do NOT** use a sealed solid bottom (creates a bathtub that drowns roots).

### Recommended Approach (Homestead and Chill, tested method)

1. Install **galvanized hardware cloth** (1/2" mesh) as a basket inside the frame, secured to inner walls with screws or U-nails
2. Layer **commercial-duty landscape fabric** on top of the hardware cloth — loose, not taut
3. Add **cross-support boards** (2x4 cedar) spanning across the bottom inside the frame at midpoints

> *"I don't recommend placing the soil itself right on the concrete. Yet I don't necessarily recommend adding a solid bottom on the bed either. Instead, we create a wire and fabric 'basket' or bottom on our raised bed to both contain the soil but also promote drainage."*
> — Deanna Talerico, Homestead and Chill

**For the 30" wide section:** Add at least one cross-board to prevent hardware cloth sagging under soil weight.
**For the 24" wide section:** Hardware cloth alone may be sufficient, but a cross-board adds insurance.

### What NOT to Do

- Do not put soil directly on concrete — it washes out, makes a mess, and concrete alkalinity will affect pH
- Do not use a completely sealed solid bottom without adequate drainage holes — creates a bathtub
- Do not use cheap landscape fabric — the thin black plastic-like variety tears quickly; use commercial-grade woven fabric

## L-Shape Construction

### Option A: Two Adjacent Rectangles (Recommended)

Build two separate rectangular beds and join them at the corner:
- **Bed 1:** 60" x 30" x 24"
- **Bed 2:** 48" x 24" x 24"

Connect them at the junction with a **shared 4x4" cedar corner post** (cut to 24" height). Attach both sections to this post with 3" exterior screws. Pre-drill to prevent splitting.

**Advantages:**
- Simpler construction (no complex corner joinery)
- Individual sections can be repaired or replaced independently
- Easier soil management if running separate pH zones (which you need)
- Can be disassembled for moving

### Option B: Single Continuous L-Frame

Build as one structure using a 4x4" corner post at the interior L-junction. Run boards from each leg to this post. Works well but is more complex.

> **UF/IFAS Extension:** *"Beds can be various shapes (e.g., square, rectangle, L shaped, triangle)"* — L-shaped beds are standard and normal.

### Critical: Interior Cross-Bracing

At 60" length with 30" width and 24" depth, the long side needs an **interior cross-brace** (2x6" board spanning the width) at the 30" midpoint to resist outward bow from wet soil hydrostatic pressure. This is critical — 24" of saturated soil exerts significant lateral force.

## Weight Calculations

### Volume Breakdown

| Section | Dimensions | Volume |
|---|---|---|
| Long section | 60" x 30" x 24" = 5' x 2.5' x 2' | **25 cu ft** |
| Short section | 48" x 24" x 24" = 4' x 2' x 2' | **16 cu ft** |
| **Total** | | **41 cu ft** |

### Weight Estimates (with 4-6" gravel drainage layer)

| Component | Estimated Weight |
|---|---|
| Gravel layer (~10 cu ft x 100 lbs/cu ft) | ~1,000 lbs |
| Soil mix (~31 cu ft, moist at ~50 lbs/cu ft) | ~1,550 lbs |
| Cedar frame | ~150–200 lbs |
| Plants, water in pore spaces | ~100 lbs |
| **Total** | **~2,800–2,900 lbs (~1.3–1.4 tons)** |

### Load Per Square Foot

- Footprint: ~20.5 sq ft
- Load: **~136–140 lbs/sq ft**

### Safety Assessment

| Surface | Rating | Safe? |
|---|---|---|
| Wooden residential deck | 40–60 PSF | **NO — too heavy for a wood deck** |
| Concrete patio (standard 4" slab) | 3,000–4,000+ PSI compressive | **YES — well within capacity** |

**Practical guidance:**
- A solid concrete patio on stable ground is fine for this weight
- If the patio is cracked, heaved, or shows subsidence, consult a structural engineer before loading ~1.4 tons
- Position the bed at the edge closer to the house (where the slab is supported by the foundation) rather than floating in the middle of a large span

## Concrete Protection

### Staining Mechanisms
1. **Tannin runoff** from cedar wood — turns concrete orange/brown
2. **Sediment/mineral runoff** from soil and compost
3. **Moss and algae** that grow in permanently moist shadow beneath the bed

### Mitigation Strategies

- **Pre-seal the concrete** under the bed footprint with a penetrating concrete sealer (Siloxa-Tek or RadonSeal) before placing the bed
- **Seal cedar bottom edges** with food-safe wood sealer (Seal-It-Green's Garden Box Armor) to reduce tannin bleed
- **Leave a 1/2" gap** under frame edges for airflow — use shims or bed feet to prevent trapped moisture and moss
- A 4–6" gravel base layer inside the bed significantly reduces soil runoff staining

### Cleanup
- Pressure washing removes most staining if you ever move the bed
- Muriatic acid solution handles stubborn mineral stains

> *"If and when you ever decide to move the garden bed, it may have some discoloration below. Yet a pressure washer should be able to remove it!"*
> — Homestead and Chill

## Thermal Considerations (Zone 7b–8a)

Concrete is a **thermal mass material** — absorbs heat slowly during the day, releases it slowly at night. This creates both benefits and risks.

### Temperature Data

| Surface | Peak Summer Temp | Behavior |
|---|---|---|
| Wood raised bed | 80–90°F exterior | Insulates soil well |
| Metal raised bed | 100–120°F exterior | Heats soil rapidly |
| Dark concrete surface | 125–140°F in direct sun | Radiates heat upward into bed base |

### Winter/Spring Benefit

Concrete absorbs solar heat and radiates it upward through the gravel base, keeping roots warmer at night. This can **extend your growing season by 2–4 weeks** in spring and fall.

### Summer Risk

Root zone temperatures above 85–90°F cause stress in most plants. Sustained temps above 95°F can kill fine feeder roots.

### Summer Heat Mitigation

1. **Mulch heavily** — 3–4" of organic mulch on the soil surface (single most effective tool)
2. **Drip irrigation** — consistent moisture provides evaporative cooling
3. **Light-colored bed material** — light cedar reflects more heat than dark metal or stained wood
4. **Evening hose-down** of surrounding concrete — evaporative cooling of the patio reduces ambient heat
5. **Strategic plant placement** — heat-sensitive plants (bluebells) on the shaded side

> **Rural Sprout:** *"Consider your concrete color. Raised beds built on dark pavement will absorb more sunlight and heat up quickly. They will have a higher evaporation rate and need more frequent watering."*

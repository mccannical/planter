## Sun Needs

**Full sun (6+ hours)** strongly preferred.

> **Missouri Botanical Garden:** *"Best flowering is in full sun, but plants generally appreciate some dappled sun in areas with hot, humid summers."*

- Minimum 4 hours for acceptable flowering (The Spruce)
- Below 6 hours: noticeably reduced bloom, leggy growth, poor air circulation (powdery mildew risk)
- In zone 8a, light afternoon dappling (not shade) can reduce heat stress without compromising bloom
- **Target:** Full morning sun through noon at minimum

---

## Water Needs

| Season | Frequency | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Early spring (Mar–Apr) | Every 5–7 days | Active bloom; soil moist but never wet |
| Late spring (May) | Every 7 days | Post-bloom recovery |
| **Summer (Jun–Aug)** | **Every 3–5 days in raised bed** | Concrete amplifies heat; check top inch — if dry, water |
| Fall (Sep–Oct) | Every 10–14 days | Cooling temps reduce demand |
| Winter (Nov–Feb) | Every 3–4 weeks if no rain | Minimal need |

> **The Spruce:** *"Creeping phlox will likely require watering weekly during the heat of the summer."*

> **Gardening Know How:** *"Water regularly but allow the soil to dry slightly between each watering. In a container, creeping phlox may rot in soggy soil."*

**Drought tolerance:** Moderate once established. Native to "somewhat dry, rocky or sandy places" (Missouri Botanical Garden). Will NOT survive sustained waterlogging.

## Soil Needs — WELL-DRAINING ZONE

**This is the plant that needs the dedicated well-draining soil section in your bed.**

### Why Drainage is Non-Negotiable

> **Missouri Botanical Garden:** *"Good soil drainage is important. Plants grow well in sandy or gravelly soils and tolerate hot, dry exposures better than most other species of phlox."*

> **Missouri Botanical Garden:** *"Native to somewhat dry, rocky or sandy places, open woodland areas and slopes."*

The species evolved on rocky Appalachian slopes with rapid water movement. **Standing water = death by root rot.** This is the #1 killer of phlox in raised beds with inadequate drainage.

### Well-Draining Soil Mix (for the phlox zone)

Create a **distinct gritty zone** in the corner/edge where phlox will live:

| Component | Proportion | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Quality loamy topsoil | 40% | Structure, some nutrition |
| Coarse horticultural grit or pea gravel (3/8") | 30% | Rapid drainage |
| Coarse perlite (chunky grade) | 20% | Drainage without compaction |
| Coarse horticultural sand (NOT builder's sand) | 10% | Additional drainage |

This mix **drains within 30–60 seconds** when watered.

### pH
- **6.0–7.0** (flexible — does NOT need acid)
- Tolerates slightly acidic to slightly alkaline
- This conflicts with azalea (4.5–5.5) — keep them in separate zones

### What It Cannot Tolerate
- Waterlogged soil even briefly
- Heavy clay without amendment
- Mulch piled against the crown (creates crown rot)

### Placement
- Position **near drainage holes** in the bed wall
- At the outer edge of the bed (cascading over the side)
- In the neutral-pH zone (short leg of the L), away from acid amendments

## Nutrient Needs

**Minimal feeder** — over-fertilizing causes problems.

> **The Spruce:** *"Fertilizing in the late winter or early spring will promote growth and support a more robust bloom. Feed with a general slow-release fertilizer suitable for flowering plants."*

### Schedule

| When | What |
|---|---|
| Late winter/early spring (Feb–Mar) | One application slow-release balanced fertilizer (10-10-10 or 5-10-5) |
| Growing season (optional) | Fish emulsion at half strength every 4–6 weeks |
| Late summer–fall | **No fertilizing** — promotes tender growth vulnerable to damage |

**Warning:** Excess nitrogen = lush leafy growth at expense of flowers. If plant fails to bloom, too much nitrogen or too much shade is often the culprit.

### Container/Raised Bed Feeding
> **Gardening Know How:** *"Feed container grown phlox every other week using a general purpose, water-soluble fertilizer mixed to half strength."*

## Care & Watering Cycle (Month by Month)

| Month | Activity |
|---|---|
| **February** | Apply slow-release fertilizer before new growth. Trim any winter-damaged brown areas. Foliage stays green through winter in zone 7b–8a. |
| **Mar–April** | **Peak bloom** — white flowers covering the mat. No fertilizer during bloom. Water regularly if dry. |
| **May** | **CRITICAL: Shear back by 1/3 to 1/2 immediately after bloom ends.** Use sharp hedge shears uniformly across the entire mat. Mat will look bare briefly, then push vigorous new growth within 2–3 weeks. |
| **Jun–Aug** | Water every 3–5 days in raised bed. Monitor for spider mites during hot/dry spells. No fertilizer. |
| **Sep–Oct** | Good time for dividing (every 2–3 years). Light compost if desired. Reduce watering. |
| **Nov–Jan** | Semi-evergreen foliage persists — attractive green texture at bed edge all winter. No protection needed. No feeding. |

### Post-Bloom Shearing — The Most Important Maintenance Task

> **Missouri Botanical Garden:** *"Cut back stems after flowering by 1/2 to maintain form and promote denser growth plus to stimulate a possible light rebloom."*

> **Gardening Know How:** *"Cut the plant back by one-third to one-half after blooming to create a neater plant and encourage a second flush of blooms."*

**Timing:** Immediately after blooms fade — typically **May in zone 7b–8a.**

**Technique:**
1. Use sharp, clean hedge shears or pruning shears
2. Shear the entire mat back by 1/3 to 1/2 — cut uniformly across the top
3. For raised bed edge plants: trim cascading runners to desired length (or let them flow freely)

**Why this matters:** Skipping post-bloom shearing leads to open, woody centers, reduced vigor, and an increasingly ragged mat. Regular shearing keeps it looking lush year after year.

**Do NOT:**
- Shear in fall or late summer (removes next year's developing growth)
- Shear while in bloom (wait until last flower fades)

## Growth Habit & Edge Planting

**Ideal for raised bed edges** — this is the plant's best use in your setup.

> **Chicago Botanic Garden:** *"It grows only 6 inches high, forming a dense cushion of dark green foliage 18 inches wide that will cascade over a wall."*

### Growth Details
- **Height:** 4–6 inches (mat-forming)
- **Spread:** 18–24 inches; 6–10 inches lateral growth per year once established
- **Cascading:** Can spill 12–18 inches down the side of a raised bed
- **Semi-evergreen:** Retains foliage through winter in zone 7b–8a — year-round texture

### Placement
- Crown **4–6 inches from the bed wall**
- Allow 18 inches of space behind for spread into the bed
- Position on the side that gets the most sun
- Will form a solid mat that cascades dramatically over the bed edge within 2–3 seasons

## Winter Hardiness (Zone 7b–8a)

**Fully hardy with no protection needed.**

Ball Seed rates 'Snowflake' for zones 3a–8b. Zone 7b–8a winter lows (10–20°F) will not harm established plants. The semi-evergreen foliage may look rough after hard freezes but regenerates in spring.

**Heat hardiness:** Actually performs better in zone 7b–8a than colder zones — native to sunny, warm-summer Appalachians. Tolerates summer heat well with adequate drainage and periodic water.

## Troubleshooting Guide

| Problem | Likely Cause | Solution |
|---|---|---|
| **Brown patches in mat** | Spider mites (webbing on undersides), root rot (mushy roots), or foliar nematodes (brown wedge patterns on leaves) | Check undersides for webbing; inspect roots; nematodes = remove/destroy plant |
| **Center die-out / bare center** | Normal aging every 3–5 years; insufficient post-bloom shearing | Divide and replant; or cut center hard and allow regrowth |
| **Failure to bloom** | Too much shade (<4 hrs sun); excess nitrogen fertilizer; needs division (4+ years) | Move to sunnier spot; use low-N fertilizer; divide |
| **Leggy, open mat** | Skipped post-bloom shearing; too much shade | Shear immediately post-bloom each year; ensure 6+ hrs sun |
| **Yellow foliage** | Overwatering or too little light | Check drainage; assess sun hours |
| **Wilting in morning** | Root rot (if soil moist) OR underwatering (if soil dry) — opposite problems | Check soil moisture and root condition |
| **Spider mites** | Hot, dry conditions (Jul–Aug) | Strong water jets to leaf undersides; insecticidal soap; neem oil |

## Pests & Diseases

### Spider Mites — Primary Pest

> **Missouri Botanical Garden:** *"Spider mites can be a problem, particularly in hot, dry conditions."*

- **Signs:** Fine webbing on undersides; stippled, bronze-looking leaves
- **Peak:** Hot, dry July–August
- **Organic treatment:** Strong water jets to undersides; insecticidal soap (avoid above 85°F); neem oil

### Foliar Nematodes

> **Missouri Botanical Garden:** *"Foliar nematodes can cause damage in wet/humid conditions."*

- **Signs:** Brown, wedge-shaped leaf patches following veins
- **Treatment:** No effective chemical control. Remove and destroy affected plants. Improve air circulation. Do not replant in same soil.

### Root Rot

> **The Spruce:** *"Spotty leaves, soft stems, and mushy roots if planted in poorly draining and excessively moist soil."*

**#1 killer in raised beds when drainage is inadequate.** Prevention = excellent drainage in soil mix. Once established, no recovery — remove and destroy.

### Powdery Mildew

> **Missouri Botanical Garden:** *"Phlox subulata and its cultivars do not seem to be bothered by powdery mildew to the same extent as most other species of phlox."*

Good news — 'Snowflake' is inherently more mildew-resistant than tall garden phlox. If it appears: potassium bicarbonate, sulfur-based fungicides (not above 85°F), or neem oil.

---

## Raised Bed Specific Considerations

1. **Drainage is everything.** Use the gritty mix described above. Position near drainage holes.
2. **Raised beds dry faster** — may need watering every 3–4 days during heat waves on concrete
3. **Edge cascade is the star feature** — let it spill over the wall for dramatic spring display
4. **No mulch against the crown** — unlike the azalea/winterberry zones, do NOT mulch around phlox crowns
5. **Divide every 2–3 years** to prevent center die-out
6. **pH neutral zone** — keep away from acid amendments intended for azalea/winterberry

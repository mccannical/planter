## Bed Profile

- **Total volume:** ~41 cu ft (~1.5 cu yards)
- **Effective soil volume** (after 4" gravel layer): ~34 cu ft
- **Location:** On concrete patio, zone 7b–8a
- **Plants:** Winterberry (acid), Azalea (acid), Virginia Bluebells (neutral), Carpet Phlox (neutral, well-draining)

---

## Why NOT Mel's Mix

Mel's Mix (1/3 compost, 1/3 peat, 1/3 vermiculite) was designed for intensive annual vegetable production in 6–12" beds. It fails for this project because:

1. **Vermiculite degrades over 3–5 years.** Woody shrubs (winterberry, azalea) live 20+ years and need long-term structural stability from mineral soil particles.
2. **No topsoil = no cation exchange capacity (CEC) foundation.** Mineral soil particles provide the base for nutrient binding and long-term fertility that blended compost alone cannot sustain.
3. **Vermiculite retains water, not promotes drainage.** For a bed on sealed concrete where drainage is the critical risk, this is a liability.
4. **UMD Extension explicitly recommends for beds on hard surfaces:** compost + soilless growing mix 1:1 — not Mel's Mix.

## pH Zone Strategy

Your plants have **incompatible pH requirements**. The L-shape is ideal for creating two distinct zones:

| Zone | Plants | Target pH | Location |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Acid Zone** | Winterberry, Azalea (x2) | 4.5–5.5 | Long leg (60" x 30") |
| **Neutral Zone** | Virginia Bluebells (x2), Carpet Phlox | 6.0–7.0 | Short leg (48" x 24") |

### Physical Separation

Use **buried physical barriers** at the junction between the two legs:
- 6–12" strips of rigid HDPE root barrier or cedar boards standing vertically
- This minimizes pH migration from one zone to the other
- Test each zone separately every spring and amend individually

### pH Drift Warning

**Concrete leaches lime and raises pH over time** — this is a direct threat to acid-loving plants. The ARS explicitly warns: *"Avoid planting azaleas near concrete sidewalks, driveways or foundations that may leach out lime which raises the pH."*

Countermeasures:
- Test pH every spring with a reliable meter
- Apply sulfur granules or acidifying fertilizer each spring
- Use rainwater for irrigation if possible (tap water often pH 7.0–8.5)
- Pine bark mulch naturally acidifies as it decomposes
- Use **limestone-free gravel** in the drainage layer (river rock, quartzite, silica-based pea gravel — NOT limestone aggregate)

## Soil Mix Recipes

### Acid Zone Mix (Winterberry + Azalea)

| Component | Proportion | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Aged blended compost | 30–40% | Nutrients, biology, moisture retention |
| Pine bark fines (1/4" or smaller) | 20–30% | Acidifies as it decomposes, drainage, suppresses Phytophthora root rot |
| Peat moss or coir | 20–25% | Acidity, moisture retention |
| Perlite or horticultural grit | 15–20% | Drainage, aeration |
| Elemental sulfur | Per soil test | Target pH 4.5–5.5 |

> **ARS:** *"Pine bark is particularly good because substances in the pine bark are thought to inhibit fungi that cause root rot."*

> **MU Extension:** *"Avoid using a large amount of peat, especially for rhododendrons, because peat-amended soil may hold excessive moisture during wet conditions in winter and spring."*

**Validated raised bed mixes from authoritative sources:**

| Mix | Source | Composition |
|---|---|---|
| Rhododendron Species Botanical Garden | ARS | 2/3 coarse sand + 1/3 medium bark |
| Everett Farwell mix | ARS | 80% medium fir bark + 20% crushed lava rock |
| Holden Arboretum mix | ARS | 5 parts coarse sand + 1 part pine bark + 4 parts loamy soil |
| MU Extension clay soil mix | MU Extension G6825 | 50% ground pine bark, 25% coarse sand, 25% topsoil |

### Neutral Zone Mix (Bluebells + general)

| Component | Proportion | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Quality loamy topsoil | 40% | Structure, CEC, neutral pH |
| Aged compost | 25% | Nutrients, organic matter |
| Coarse horticultural grit or pea gravel | 20% | Drainage |
| Coarse perlite (chunky grade) | 15% | Drainage without compaction |

### Well-Draining Sub-Zone (Phlox specifically)

The phlox evolved on rocky slopes — it needs the fastest-draining soil in the bed. Create a dedicated pocket:

| Component | Proportion | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Quality loamy topsoil | 40% | Structure, some nutrition |
| Coarse horticultural grit or pea gravel (3/8") | 30% | Rapid drainage |
| Coarse perlite (chunky grade) | 20% | Drainage without compaction |
| Coarse horticultural sand (NOT builder's sand) | 10% | Additional drainage |

This mix **drains within 30–60 seconds** when watered and does not compact over time.

## Organic Matter Recommendations

> **UMD Extension:** *"The organic matter (OM) content in a raised bed should be 25% to 50% by volume or 5% to 15% by weight."*

### Priority Amendments

1. **Aged/blended compost** — The gold standard. Should be from 3–5 diverse sources for broad nutrient profile. Ensure it is mature and does not carry excess soluble salts (Iowa State warning).

2. **Pine bark fines** — Superior for acid-loving plants. Decomposes slowly to humic acids, improves drainage structure. Use particle sizes ≤1/4" for raised bed mixing (larger bark is for surface mulch, not amendment). Critical for the azalea/winterberry zone.

3. **Leaf mold** — Partially decomposed deciduous leaves. Holds 3x its weight in water. Mild acidity (pH ~6.0), rich in fungal networks beneficial to ericaceous plants. Oak leaf mold is more acidic than maple.

### Avoid in This Project

- **Fresh wood chips** — tie up nitrogen during decomposition
- **Lime or limestone** — any addition fights your acid goals
- **Composted manure as primary material** — tends toward neutral-alkaline pH 7.0–7.5
- **Aluminum sulfate** — toxic to azalea roots (confirmed by ARS and MU Extension)

## Amendments: Moisture Retention vs. Drainage

| Amendment | Improves Drainage | Retains Moisture | Best Use |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Perlite** | Strong | Low | Drainage, aeration |
| **Vermiculite** | Moderate | Strong | Moisture retention (use sparingly) |
| **Coarse sand** (horticultural) | Good | Low | Drainage structure |
| **Coir (coconut fiber)** | Moderate | Good | Moisture + neutral pH |
| **Peat moss** | Moderate | Strong | Moisture + acidity |
| **Pine bark fines** | Good | Moderate | Acid drainage structure |
| **Biochar** | Moderate | Moderate | Long-term soil structure |

**For your concrete-sited bed:** Prioritize drainage over moisture retention. Beds on concrete face waterlogging risk during heavy rain; the soil must drain actively.

## Acidification Protocol

> **Oregon State Extension:** *"Apply elemental sulfur or aluminum sulfate: Start by top-dressing the soil around the plant with 1/3 pound of elemental sulfur or 1 pound of aluminum sulfate. Repeat monthly applications until you reach a total of 1.5 pounds of sulfur per plant."*

**NOTE:** For azaleas, use **elemental sulfur or ferrous sulfate ONLY** — aluminum sulfate is toxic to azalea/rhododendron roots.

### pH Correction Table (from MU Extension G6825)

| Initial pH | Sulfur (lbs/100 sq ft) | Iron Sulfate (lbs/100 sq ft) |
|---|---|---|
| 7.5 | 5.0 | 11.5 |
| 7.0 | 3.5 | 9.0 |
| 6.5 | 1.5 | 3.5 |

### Preferred Acidifying Organic Amendments (ranked)

1. **Pine bark fines** — slow-release humic acids, long-lasting
2. **Sphagnum peat moss** — pH ~3.5–4.5 itself
3. **Sulfur-amended compost** — mix elemental sulfur into compost before filling
4. **Cottonseed meal** — mild acidifying fertilizer, adds nitrogen
5. **Coffee grounds** (as amendment, not mulch) — mild acidifier, use in moderation

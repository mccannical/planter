## Sun Needs

**Morning sun with afternoon shade** is the critical siting decision in zone 7b–8a.

> **Monrovia:** *"Shelter from harsh sun exposures in hot summer areas."*

> **MU Extension G6825:** *"Filtered sunlight is ideal, but morning sunlight with shade after 1 PM is satisfactory."*

> **ARS:** *"In hot areas, northern exposures are preferable to southern exposures."*

### Zone 7b–8a Reality

Mandarin Lights was bred for **extreme cold tolerance** (Minnesota), not Southeast summer heat. Hot afternoon sun is the primary stressor — causes lace bug infestations, leaf scorch, and flower bud bleaching.

**Recommended:**
- Full morning sun (6 AM–12/1 PM) — essential for flowering
- Light shade or dappled shade in afternoon (1–5 PM) — especially when temps exceed 90°F
- Position on the north or east side of taller plants, or where the bed structure provides afternoon relief

**Important nuance:** As a **deciduous** azalea, Mandarin Lights is more sun-tolerant than large-leaved evergreen rhododendrons. Zone 7b can likely handle full sun. Zone 8a — afternoon shade is insurance.

## Water Needs

**Highly sensitive to both over- AND under-watering.**

> **ARS:** *"Rhododendrons and azaleas ideally grow in damp, never soggy soil. Think of taking a wet sponge and squeezing it. The remaining damp sponge is the nearly perfect air and water environment for rhododendron roots."*

### The Morning Droop Test

- Leaves droop in afternoon but recover overnight → **Normal**
- Leaves still droop at dawn → **Water immediately**
- Leaves wilt in morning AND soil IS moist → **Root rot — usually fatal**

### Seasonal Watering Schedule

| Season | Frequency | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Spring bloom (Mar–Apr) | Every 5–7 days | Support flowering; damp not wet |
| Post-bloom growth (May–Jun) | Every 5–7 days | Consistent moisture for new foliage |
| **July–August (heat)** | **Every 2–3 days in raised bed** | Raised beds on concrete dry rapidly; finger test 2" depth daily |
| September | Every 10–14 days | **Reduce deliberately** — allow hardening for winter |
| Oct–Nov | Every 2–3 weeks | One deep watering after first frost if fall was dry |
| Winter | Monthly if no rain | Roots need some moisture even when dormant |

### Establishment (Year 1)

> **Monrovia:** *"Water deeply, regularly in first growing season to establish root system."*

> **ARS:** *"After transplanting the roots can take longer to grow out into the surrounding soil... if the root ball is allowed to dry out it can be difficult to get it wet again."*

In a raised bed on concrete, water more frequently in year 1 since roots cannot reach groundwater. Dripping hose at the base for 2–3 hours during dry spells.

## Soil Needs — pH Is the Central Issue

### pH: Strict Acid Requirement

> **MU Extension G6825:** *"Rhododendrons and azaleas must have an acid soil. Most of them thrive best at a soil pH between 5.0 and 5.5."*

> **ARS:** *"Rhododendrons and azaleas prefer acidic soils having a pH between 4.5 to 6.0."*

**Target: pH 4.5–5.5.** At pH 6.0+, iron and manganese become chemically unavailable → chlorosis. At pH 7.0+, plant will decline.

### Concrete pH Drift — Direct Threat

> **ARS:** *"Avoid planting azaleas near concrete sidewalks, driveways or foundations that may leach out lime which raises the pH."*

Your concrete patio WILL leach lime into the bed over time. **Test pH annually and amend proactively.**

### Soil Composition

> **ARS:** *"About half of the planting medium should be organic material. Combinations of sphagnum peat moss, pine or fir bark fines, compost, and aged, chopped leaves should be worked into the soil."*

**Pine bark is critical:**
> **ARS:** *"Pine bark is particularly good because substances in the pine bark are thought to inhibit fungi that cause root rot."*

> **MU Extension:** *"Avoid using a large amount of peat, especially for rhododendrons, because peat-amended soil may hold excessive moisture during wet conditions."*

### Drainage — Also Strict

> **ARS:** *"Planting in raised beds is the best solution in heavy soils."*

Your raised bed setup is actually the **ARS-recommended approach** for azalea cultivation. The key is ensuring the bed drains well.

### What NOT to Use

- **Aluminum sulfate** — TOXIC to azalea roots (confirmed ARS and MU Extension)
- General garden fertilizers — use acid-specific only
- Lime or limestone in any form
- Tap water if pH > 7.5 (use rainwater instead)

## Nutrient Needs — Azalea-Specific Protocol

> **ARS:** *"Unlike other shrubs, azaleas are shallow rooted and can be easily injured by excess fertilizer. Some experienced growers do not apply chemical fertilizers at all."*

### What to Use

> **MU Extension:** *"Avoid using general garden fertilizers. Use those specially formulated for acid-loving plants. Fertilizers supplying nitrogen in the ammonium form are best."*

**Best products:**
- Holly-Tone (Espoma) — organic granular, industry standard
- Azalea/Camellia/Rhododendron fertilizers
- Cottonseed meal — organic nitrogen, recommended by ARS and MU Extension
- Fish emulsion — good organic liquid option

> **MU Extension:** *"A fertilizer analysis similar to 6-10-4 applied at 2 pounds per 100 square feet is usually adequate."*

### Schedule

| When | Action |
|---|---|
| March–April | Light application as buds swell (supports bloom) |
| Post-bloom (May) | Main annual feeding with acid fertilizer |
| **STOP by July 1** | Late feeding promotes tender growth vulnerable to heat AND winter damage |
| Late fall (optional) | 1.25 cups iron sulfate per 100 sq ft for winter hardening and chlorosis prevention |

### Iron and Sulfur Supplementation

> **ARS:** *"Lack of iron causes yellowing between green-colored veins on younger leaves. Iron deficiency is frequently caused by too high a soil pH."*

- Annual elemental sulfur or ferrous (iron) sulfate to maintain pH below 5.5
- **Do NOT use aluminum sulfate** — toxic to azalea roots

### Phosphorus Myth

> **ARS:** *"The popular perception that rhododendron and azalea flowering is enhanced by phosphorus is incorrect."*

Don't over-apply phosphorus. Stick to acid-specific formulas.

## Root System & Mulching

### Shallow Fibrous Roots

> **ARS:** *"Rhododendrons and azaleas have no tap roots. They have fibrous roots — a dense mass of hair-like roots."*

> **ARS:** *"Because of their shallow roots, little or no cultivation should be done around rhododendrons. Weeds should be carefully pulled."*

### Mulching — Critical Practice

> **MU Extension:** *"Most azaleas and rhododendrons are shallow rooted and need a heavy layer of mulch to conserve moisture and minimize winter injury."*

**Best mulch materials:**
- Pine bark chips or fines (preferred by ARS — inhibits Phytophthora)
- Pine needles (classic azalea mulch — maintains acidity)
- Partly decomposed oak leaves (naturally acidifying)

**Depth:** 2–3" year-round.

**Critical rule:**
> **ARS:** *"Do not pile the mulch right up to the trunk, leave 5–6 inches free of mulch."*

Mulch against the crown = crown rot = plant death.

> **MU Extension:** *"Mulch should be moved away from direct contact with stems in early fall to allow hardening."*

## Care & Watering Cycle (Month by Month)

| Month | Activity |
|---|---|
| **Feb–Mar** | Leafless but flower buds developing. Light acid fertilizer as buds swell. Do NOT prune. Ensure moisture if winter was dry. |
| **Mar–Apr** | **Peak bloom** — mandarin orange flowers erupt before leaves. Water regularly. No fertilizer during bloom. |
| **Apr–May** | **Primary pruning window** — immediately after bloom. Main annual fertilizer application. Deadhead spent clusters. New foliage emerges. |
| **May–Jun** | Active growth. Maintain consistent moisture. Light shaping if desired (finish before July). Monitor for lace bugs. Final fertilizer deadline: **July 1.** |
| **Jul–Aug** | Heat stress phase. **No fertilizing.** Water deeply 2–3x/week. Morning-only irrigation (wet foliage overnight → disease). Monitor for spider mites and lace bugs. |
| **Sep–Oct** | Reduce watering — allow hardening. Apply fresh 2–3" mulch (pine bark/needles). No fertilizer. Pull mulch away from stems. |
| **November** | One deep watering after first frost if fall was dry. Replenish mulch. |
| **Dec–Jan** | Fully dormant and leafless. Flower buds visible as small nubs. No watering unless drought. Monitor mulch depth. |

### Pruning

> **ARS:** *"Rhododendrons and azaleas are pruned after they bloom. Pruning after buds are set removes next year's flowers."*

- **Window:** Immediately after bloom (April–May) through early summer — **never after July 1**
- Deciduous azaleas can be cut anywhere and will branch from that point
- Pinch soft new shoots for denser form (before July)
- Deadhead spent flower clusters for appearance and disease prevention
- **Rejuvenation:** If leggy after many years, remove 1/3 of old wood per year over 3 years

## Winter Hardiness & Zone 7b–8a Heat

### Cold: Non-Issue

> **MU Extension:** *"Plants and flower buds are said to have survived -40 degrees Fahrenheit."*

Zone 7b minimum temps (5–10°F) are nothing to this plant. Winter cold is NOT the challenge.

### Heat: The Actual Challenge

Mandarin Lights was bred for cold, not heat. Zone 8a summer heat (90–100°F) is the primary stressor:

1. Lace bug pressure increases in sunny, hot exposures
2. Phytophthora root rot is more aggressive in hot, wet conditions
3. Flower bud bleaching from intense summer sun
4. Summer leaf scorch from heat + wind + drying

### Heat Mitigation

- Afternoon shade (the single most impactful strategy)
- Deep organic mulch (3") to keep roots cool
- Morning-only irrigation — never wet foliage at night in summer
- Pine bark in soil mix (suppresses Phytophthora)

> **ARS:** *"In hot climates, raised planting beds that incorporate 50% or more fine pine bark can be helpful in suppressing Phytophthora root rot."*

Since Mandarin Lights is **deciduous**, winter sun/wind desiccation of foliage is NOT an issue (no leaves to protect). This is an advantage over evergreen rhododendrons.

## Troubleshooting Guide

### Chlorosis (Yellowing Leaves)

| Pattern | Cause | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Young leaves yellow, veins green | Iron deficiency (pH too high) | Test pH; apply elemental sulfur; chelated iron foliar spray for immediate relief |
| Old leaves yellow, young green | Magnesium deficiency | Epsom salts (1 tbsp/gallon) |
| All leaves uniformly pale | Overall nutrient deficiency or pH too low | Soil test |

> **MU Extension:** *"If the leaves turn yellow in sections between the veins, but the veins remain green, iron deficiency is the cause."*

> **Purdue Extension:** *"Iron chlorosis can usually be temporarily controlled by spraying foliage with iron sulfate solution at one ounce per gallon of water."*

### Other Problems

| Problem | Likely Cause | Solution |
|---|---|---|
| **Wilting in morning, soil IS moist** | **Phytophthora root rot — usually fatal** | Improve drainage immediately; phosphonate fungicides if caught early |
| **Bud blast (buds die without opening)** | Late spring freeze; drought during bud formation (Aug–Oct) | Protected placement; adequate moisture during bud-setting |
| **Leaf scorch (brown edges/tips)** | Too much afternoon sun; drought; wind | Afternoon shade; deep mulch; consistent moisture |
| **Lace bugs** | Sunny, hot exposure (#1 pest in zone 7b–8a) | Insecticidal soap; neem oil; plant in morning-sun/afternoon-shade |
| **Branch dieback** | Phomopsis/Botryosphaeria fungi (drought stress) | Prune well below diseased area with sterilized tools |
| **Powdery mildew** | Cool moist weather, poor air circulation | Potassium bicarbonate; improve air circulation |
| **Failure to bloom** | Pruned after July 1; late frost; too much shade; excess nitrogen | Time pruning correctly; 4+ hrs sun; reduce nitrogen |
| **Weevils (leaf edge notches)** | Adult weevils feeding at night | Entomopathogenic nematodes in soil for larvae |

## Pests & Diseases

### Lace Bugs — Primary Pest

> **ARS:** *"Both adults and nymphs feed on the backside of the leaf resulting in yellow spots. Lace bugs thrive in sunny locations since their natural enemies avoid these locations."*

- **Identification:** Flip leaf — lace-winged adults or dark excrement on underside; yellow stippling on top
- **Organic treatment:** Insecticidal soap (nymphs); neem oil; forceful water spray to undersides; plant in morning-sun/afternoon-shade

### Phytophthora Root Rot — Fatal Disease

> **ARS:** *"Root rot is usually caused by Phytophthora. If leaves are wilted in the morning and the ground is moist, this is a symptom. It is usually fatal."*

- **Prevention is everything:** Excellent drainage; pine bark in soil; never overwater
- **Early stage only:** Phosphonate fungicides (Aliette, Agri-Fos)

### Branch Die-back

> **ARS:** *"Die-back of entire branches is usually caused by Phomopsis or Botryosphaeria. These fungi thrive during dry periods when plants are stressed."*

- Prune dead/dying branches well below disease. Dispose in trash, not compost. Disinfect pruners between cuts.

### Powdery Mildew

> **ARS:** *"The white powder form is prevalent on deciduous azaleas."*

- Potassium bicarbonate (Bonide Remedy) — ARS-recommended
- Sulfur-based fungicides (not above 85°F)
- Improve air circulation; avoid overhead watering

### Weevils

> **ARS:** *"If leaves have notches in the outside edges, these are caused by weevils."*

- Biological control: Entomopathogenic nematodes in soil for larvae

### Spider Mites
- Hot, dry summer conditions
- Insecticidal soap or neem oil; strong water jets to leaf undersides

## pH Drift Prevention Protocol (Annual)

This requires **active management** when growing azaleas on concrete.

> **ARS:** *"Rhododendrons and azaleas will let you know if the pH is not correct."*

### Annual Checklist

1. **Test pH** (early spring) with a reliable meter
2. **If pH 5.5–6.0:** Apply wettable sulfur OR 1.25 cups iron sulfate per 100 sq ft (MU Extension)
3. **If pH above 6.0:** More aggressive correction with elemental sulfur (see table)
4. **Maintain acidic mulch** — pine needles and oak leaves naturally acidify
5. **Monitor water chemistry** — if tap water pH > 7.5, collect rainwater for irrigation

### Sulfur Application Table (MU Extension G6825)

| Current pH | Sulfur (lbs/100 sq ft) | Iron Sulfate (lbs/100 sq ft) |
|---|---|---|
| 7.5 | 5.0 | 11.5 |
| 7.0 | 3.5 | 9.0 |
| 6.5 | 1.5 | 3.5 |

## Raised Bed Specific Considerations

1. **Raised beds are the ARS-recommended approach** for azaleas in challenging conditions — you're doing it right
2. **More frequent watering** than in-ground — confirmed by ARS
3. **pH drift monitoring** is mandatory — concrete leaches lime
4. **Soil doesn't self-replenish** — top-dress with 1–2" composted pine bark every fall
5. **Root ball management** — loosen roots at planting, score vertical cuts, pull outer roots outward (ARS emphasizes this)
6. **Never plant too deep** — top of root ball should be **several inches above** surrounding soil (ARS)
7. **Temperature extremes** — raised bed on concrete gets very hot in summer; deep mulching (3") essential
8. **Mature size (4–5' tall)** — becomes the visual centerpiece. Position toward back/center of bed.
9. **24" depth is adequate** — shallow fibrous roots (12–24") fit well

## Companion Planting

### Good Companions (similar pH/drainage)

- Heuchera (coral bells) — acid-tolerant
- Epimedium — great in partial-shade azalea zone
- Leucothoe fontanesiana — same acid soil, evergreen, native
- Blueberries — identical pH needs
- Native ferns — love acid organic soil and dappled shade
- Winterberry — same acid zone in your bed

### Conflict in Your Bed

- **Phlox subulata** (pH 6.0–7.0) — keep in separate neutral zone
- **Virginia Bluebells** (pH 6.5–7.5) — keep in separate neutral zone

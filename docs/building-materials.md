## System Overview

DESIGNFORMS is a large-format modular concrete panel system. Panels are mechanically fastened with stainless steel hardware — no mortar, no adhesive. The system uses two panel sizes and a hardware kit.

### Panel Dimensions

| Part        | Height (wall height per course) | Length (along wall) | Thickness (wall depth) |
| ----------- | ------------------------------- | ------------------- | ---------------------- |
| Large panel | 12"                             | 24"                 | 2.6"                   |
| Small panel | 6"                              | 24"                 | 2.6"                   |

## Caps

DESIGNFORMS does not have a separate cap product. Belgard's own project guides use two capping methods:

### Option A: Concrete Cap (same panels laid flat)

Use the same DESIGNFORMS panels rotated 90° and laid flat on top of the wall as a ledge. A small panel (6" wide) laid flat creates a 6" wide cap ledge at 2.6" thick — gives a clean concrete edge to lean on while gardening. A large panel (12" wide) creates a wider ledge suitable for sitting or setting tools.

- Pros: matches the wall perfectly, no additional materials, clean modern look
- Cons: adds 2.6" to total wall height, heavy
- Used in Belgard's "Concrete Top" project configurations

### Option B: Wood Cap

Cedar or composite boards fastened to the top of the panel walls via the hardware brackets. This is what Belgard uses in their "Wood Cap" project kits.

- Pros: warmer feel, easier to lean on, lighter, can overhang walls for visual interest
- Cons: wood degrades over time, different material from walls
- Material: cedar 2x6 or 2x8 boards, cut to fit perimeter
- Used in Belgard's "Wood Cap" project configurations (Lowes sells DESIGNFORMS project kits with wood caps)

### Option C: No Cap (open top)

Leave the top course of wall panels as-is. Common for garden beds where the cap is purely aesthetic.

## Alternative Estimate: Standard CMU (Cinder Block)

Standard concrete masonry units as a budget alternative to DESIGNFORMS. Available at Home Depot, Lowes, and masonry supply yards.

### References

- https://www.homedepot.com/c/ab/standard-cinder-block-dimensions/9ba683603be9fa5395fab9012213e530
- https://www.archtoolbox.com/cmu-sizes-shapes-finishes/

### CMU Dimensions

Nominal dimensions include a 3/8" mortar joint. Actual block is 3/8" smaller in each direction. Everything in CMU construction works on an **8" module** — 8" tall courses, 16" long blocks (2 × 8"), 8" half blocks.

| Block | Nominal (D x H x L) | Actual (D x H x L) | Use |
|---|---|---|---|
| 8" Full Block | 8" x 8" x 16" | 7-5/8" x 7-5/8" x 15-5/8" | Standard wall unit |
| 8" Half Block | 8" x 8" x 8" | 7-5/8" x 7-5/8" x 7-5/8" | Corners, wall ends |
| 4" Solid Cap Block | 4" x 8" x 16" | 3-5/8" x 7-5/8" x 15-5/8" | Top cap course |

### Why 8" CMU

| Size | Wall Thickness | Stability at 24" | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| 4" | 3-5/8" | Marginal | Too thin at 3 courses without reinforcement |
| 6" | 5-5/8" | Adequate | Lighter, but less common at box stores |
| **8"** | **7-5/8"** | **Good** | **Standard for garden beds. Everywhere. Cheapest.** |

### Wall Height

3 courses × 8" nominal = **24" nominal wall height** — exact match for this bed.

- Actual block height: 3 × 7-5/8" + 2 × 3/8" mortar joints = 23-5/8"
- With 4" solid cap block on top: 23-5/8" + 3-5/8" = **27-1/4"** total
- Without cap: 23-5/8" (~24")

### Assembly Method

Two options for a garden bed:

| Method | Pros | Cons |
|---|---|---|
| **Dry-stack + adhesive** | No mortar mixing, easy DIY, can disassemble later | Less rigid, adhesive costs add up |
| **Mortared (running bond)** | Strongest, most permanent, traditional look | Requires mortar skill, can't easily undo |

For dry-stack: use landscape block adhesive (e.g. Loctite PL 500) between each course. Stack blocks straight (no stagger needed). Simpler than running bond.

For mortared: stagger each course by 8" (half a block). Use Type S mortar. Alternate corner blocks between courses for interlocking.

### Footprint Impact — The Big Tradeoff

8" CMU walls are **7-5/8" thick** vs. DESIGNFORMS at 2.6". For the same interior planting area, the CMU bed takes much more patio space:

| Section | Interior | DESIGNFORMS exterior | 8" CMU exterior |
|---|---|---|---|
| Long side | 60" x 30" | ~65" x 35" | ~76" x 46" |
| Short side | 18" x 24" | ~23" x 29" | ~34" x 40" |

The 8" CMU long side footprint is **76" x 46"** vs. 65" x 35" for DESIGNFORMS — an extra ~11" in each direction. If patio space is tight, this matters.

### Block Count

Same L-shape perimeter (216" interior). Each full block covers 16" of wall.

| Wall | Length | Full blocks | Half blocks | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Back | 60" | 3 | 1 | Half + 4" cut, or cut 1 full to 12" |
| Right of long side | 30" | 1 | 1 | Half + 6" cut |
| Step | 36" | 2 | 0 | + cut 1 full to 4" (wasteful) |
| Right of short side | 18" | 1 | 0 | + cut 1 full to 2" (wasteful) |

The 36" and 18" walls produce especially awkward small offcuts (4" and 2"). Offcuts from longer walls can sometimes fill shorter ones — plan cut order to minimize waste.

### CMU Shapes to Consider

Beyond standard stretcher blocks, useful shapes for a garden bed:

- **Double corner blocks** — clean finished look on exposed corners (both faces smooth)
- **Cap blocks (4" solid)** — flat solid blocks for the top course
- **Half blocks** — factory-cut halves are cleaner than field-cutting full blocks

Standard gray CMU can be painted with masonry paint for a cleaner look, or parged with stucco for a smoother finish.

### CMU Notes

- **pH leaching:** CMU is standard concrete with portland cement — it will leach lime and raise pH over time, same concern as DESIGNFORMS but with thicker walls = more surface contact with soil. Budget for more aggressive sulfur amendments in the acid zone.
- **Drainage holes:** Leave mortar joints open at the base course (every 2–3 blocks) instead of drilling. Or drill through blocks with a masonry bit. Hollow-core blocks also allow some vertical drainage through the cores.
- **Thermal mass:** 8" CMU stores significantly more heat than 2.6" DESIGNFORMS panels. On a concrete patio in zone 7b–8a, this means the bed interior gets hotter in summer. More mulch, more water.
- **Weight:** 8" CMU weighs ~33–38 lbs each. 62 blocks ≈ 2,000–2,350 lbs of block alone (vs. ~750–875 lbs for DESIGNFORMS panels). Combined with soil and gravel, total bed weight will be higher — verify patio slab capacity.

## Notes

### Concrete vs. Cedar — Design Decision

The [raised-bed-construction.md](./raised-bed-construction.md) file discusses cedar and ACQ lumber. DESIGNFORMS concrete panels are an alternative with different tradeoffs:

| Factor         | Cedar Lumber             | DESIGNFORMS Concrete                          |
| -------------- | ------------------------ | --------------------------------------------- |
| Insulation     | Better (80–90°F surface) | Concrete heats up more, but 2.6" wall is thin |
| Lifespan       | 10–20 years              | 25+ years (concrete doesn't rot)              |
| Assembly       | Screws and corner posts  | Hardware kit brackets (no mortar)             |
| Aesthetics     | Warm, natural            | Clean, modern, grey                           |
| Drainage holes | Drill through wood       | Drill through concrete (masonry bit)          |
| Modification   | Easy to cut/modify       | Harder to cut (need masonry saw)              |

### Cutting Panels

Panels can be cut with a masonry saw (wet saw or angle grinder with diamond blade). Wear eye and ear protection, work outdoors, wet-cut to control dust.

### pH Consideration

Unlike poured concrete walls or blocks with limestone aggregate, DESIGNFORMS panels are high-strength concrete. They will still leach some lime over time, which raises soil pH — the same concern documented in [soil-composition.md](./soil-composition.md). The 2.6" wall thickness means less surface area in contact with soil compared to thick-walled block construction, but annual pH testing and sulfur amendments remain essential for the acid zone.

### Drainage Holes

Weep holes must be drilled through the concrete panels (see [drainage-engineering.md](./drainage-engineering.md)). Use a 1–1.5" masonry/hammer drill bit. Pre-drill pilot holes. Position 1–2" above the base, every 12–16" around the perimeter.

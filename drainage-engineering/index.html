    Drainage Engineering for Raised Beds on Concrete¶

    Home | Layout | Construction | Build Guide | Materials | Soil | Drainage | Checklist | Winterberry | Azalea | Bluebells | Phlox

    The Core Problem¶

    On concrete, water has nowhere to go — it cannot infiltrate the ground. It must either be absorbed by the soil, evaporate, or run off across the patio surface. Two opposing risks exist simultaneously:

    1. Root rot if the bottom of the bed pools and stays saturated
    2. Rapid drying because there's no capillary moisture wicking from the ground

    "Once the raised bed dries out, there's no moisture below to wick up. There's no banking system from which to draw up moisture." — Amy Stross, Tenth Acre Farm

    The Science: Perched Water Table¶

    This is foundational to all drainage decisions.

    What It Is¶

    When you water a bed, gravity pulls water down. But near the bottom, capillary forces equal gravity, creating a permanently saturated zone called the perched water table. Its height is determined by soil particle size — not bed height or shape.

    "The size and shape of the pot do not affect the height of the perched water table." — Robert Pavlis, Garden Myths (citing soil science literature)

    Key Principles¶

    • Smaller particles = taller saturated zone. Heavy peat or clay mixes create tall saturated zones.
    • Coarser particles = shorter saturated zone. Perlite, pine bark fines, and grit reduce it.
    • Your 24" depth is protective. Even with a 4–6" saturated base zone, the top 18"+ of well-structured soil provides the aerated root zone your plants need.
    • Mix amendments throughout the soil column — never layer them. Iowa State Extension warns that distinct layers of different soil types impede water movement and discourage root growth.

    How to Minimize It¶

    "You can decrease the perched water table in any soil by increasing the average particle size. Adding sand, perlite, or vermiculite to most potting soil will accomplish this. The key is to mix it in with the soil, not to lay it at the bottom." — Garden Myths

    The Gravel Layer Debate — Settled Science (2024)¶

    The Original "Myth"¶

    WSU Extension (Linda Chalker-Scott, Ph.D.) demonstrated that gravel layers at the bottom of containers don't improve drainage and can actually retain MORE moisture in the soil above:

    "Nearly 100 years ago, soil scientists demonstrated that water does not move easily from layers of finer textured materials to layers of more coarse textured."

    The 2024 Update¶

    A peer-reviewed study by Avery Rowe (PLOS ONE, 2024–2025) showed drainage material does reduce water in the soil column, but with critical caveats:

    • Effects were greatest in highly porous soilless mixes (coir/perlite blends)
    • A 60mm (~2.3") sand layer was most effective at reducing water retention
    • Thicker layers outperformed thinner ones
    • The effect does NOT eliminate the perched water table — just reduces it

    The Critical Distinction for Raised Beds¶

    "A 'drainage layer' of coarse material can create a zone where water can move more freely into if and only if there is a way for that water to escape beyond the bed." — Deep Green Permaculture (April 2025, citing CSU, Iowa State, NDSU, OSU Extension)

    Bottom line:

    Approach Verdict
    Gravel layer with NO drain outlets Mostly counterproductive — just moves saturated zone higher
    Gravel layer WITH weep holes/drain pipes Beneficial — provides reservoir and pathway for exit
    60mm+ fine sand layer with drain outlets Best at reducing water retention per 2024 research
    Mixing amendments throughout soil Most effective overall — reduces perched water table height

    Recommended Drainage Architecture¶

    CONCRETE PATIO (raise bed 1/2–1" with spacer blocks for air gap)
    |
    v

    — 3" perforated PVC pipe embedded in gravel, routed to weep holes
    |
    v
NON-WOVEN GEOTEXTILE FABRIC (heavy-duty, not cheap woven poly)
    |
    v
SOIL MIX (16–18" effective depth)
    |
    v
2–3" ORGANIC MULCH SURFACE LAYER

    Why Each Layer Matters¶

    1. Air gap (spacer blocks): Allows water exiting weep holes to flow laterally under the bed to the patio edge. Without this gap, the bottom forms a sealed pool.

    2. Gravel layer: Creates a drainage reservoir. Combined with weep holes, it provides a pathway for excess water to exit.

    3. Perforated PVC pipe: Collects water from the gravel layer and routes it to exit points. Functions as a miniature French drain within the bed.

    4. Geotextile fabric: Prevents fine soil particles from migrating into gravel and clogging it over time. Use non-woven (not woven polypropylene — that clogs faster). Place between drainage layer and soil only, not against the concrete.

    5. Soil mix: Must drain freely — high-porosity medium is essential for beds on concrete.

    6. Mulch: Slows surface water infiltration during heavy rain (meters water entry), retains soil moisture during dry periods, moderates soil temperature.

    Weep Holes — Mandatory¶

    Drill 1–1.5" diameter holes through the bed walls: - Position at 1–2" above the base of the bed - Space every 12–16" around the entire perimeter - Minimum 4 holes per long side - Prioritize outer corners and longest walls - Angle holes slightly downward if the wall material is thick - For the L-shaped bed, ensure holes on both the interior and exterior walls

    Why Not at the Very Bottom?¶

    Raising the holes slightly (1–2" from floor level) creates a thin water reservoir below them. This protects roots from extreme drought during light rain periods while still preventing sustained waterlogging.

    Drainage Pipe Options¶

    Option A: Perimeter Weep Holes Only (Simplest)¶

    Just the drilled holes described above. Adequate for most situations. Water accumulates in the gravel layer and exits laterally through the holes onto the patio.

    Option B: Slotted Drain Pipe Grid (Most Robust)¶

    • Lay 3–4" perforated PVC drain pipe (Schedule 40 or corrugated agricultural tile) in a grid or diagonal pattern across the bed base
    • Pipe sits on concrete, covered by gravel layer, then soil above
    • Connect pipe to a single outlet exiting through the bed wall
    • Outlet should aim toward a patio drain or off the edge of the patio
    • This mimics a French drain adapted for a raised bed

    Landscape Fabric Between Gravel and Soil¶

    Pros¶

    • Prevents fine soil particles from migrating into gravel layer and clogging it ("piping" in drainage engineering)
    • Extends the functional life of the drainage layer
    • Allows water to pass through while filtering solids

    Cons¶

    • Over time (3–5+ years), it clogs with organic matter and fine particles, reducing water flow
    • Roots can penetrate and become damaged at the boundary
    • Creates a hard boundary complicating future bed renovation

    Best Practice¶

    Use non-woven geotextile fabric — not the cheap woven polypropylene sold in garden centers. Place it between the drainage layer and soil only (not against the concrete base).

    Alternative: Some practitioners layer 1" of pea gravel at the very base, then the main gravel, then geotextile, then soil. Silt bypassing the fabric settles in the pea gravel rather than clogging the main drainage.

    Wicking Beds vs. Free-Draining — NOT for This Project¶

    Wicking beds intentionally create a water reservoir at the base (6–12" deep) with soil wicking moisture upward. This is the opposite of what your plants need:

    • Virginia Bluebells go dormant in summer — a wicking bed's constant moisture would rot dormant corms
    • Carpet Phlox requires well-drained, even rocky/lean soil — highly susceptible to root rot in wet conditions
    • Winterberry tolerates wet but still benefits from good drainage structure
    • Azaleas — Phytophthora root rot is their #1 killer and thrives in saturated conditions

    Free-draining bed with active drainage outlets is the correct architecture.

    Preventing Waterlogging on Flat Concrete¶

    When the concrete has no slope to a drain, you must engineer the path for water exit.

    Priority Checklist¶

    1. Raise the bed slightly off concrete — 1/2–1" air gap with spacer blocks or feet allows water to flow laterally under the bed to the patio edge
    2. Drill weep holes at base of walls — see above
    3. Install perforated drain pipe in base — for maximum insurance
    4. Grade concrete if possible — even 1% slope (1/8" per foot) under the bed helps enormously. Shim the bed to create this slope if the patio is flat
    5. High-porosity soil mix — the medium itself must drain freely
    6. Overflow notch at top of wall — for catastrophic rain events, a notched board or lowered section at the top of the bed wall allows water to escape via overflow rather than saturating the entire soil column

    Pre-Placement Test¶

    Before building: pour 5+ gallons of water at the planned bed location and observe where it flows. If it pools with no exit path, find a better spot or create a drainage channel.

    Rain Management (Zone 7b–8a)¶

    Zone 7b–8a receives significant spring and fall rainfall. Engineering for this is essential.

    Strategies¶

    1. Overflow escape route is mandatory. If base drain holes become overwhelmed, excess water must exit via overflow notches before reaching plant crowns.

    2. Monitor drain hole performance. After heavy rain, check whether the bed drains within 1–2 hours. If saturated more than 4 hours, the system needs additional holes or pipe capacity.

    3. Mulch surface layer — 2–3" organic mulch slows surface water infiltration, reducing the "dump" of water that overwhelms drain pipes during downpours. It meters water entry.

    4. Seasonal alignment — Virginia Bluebells go dormant by early summer, perfectly aligned with zone 7b–8a's wet spring season. They're dormant when summer drought/irrigation management is needed for other plants.

    5. Temporary rain diversion — During sustained heavy rain, a simple polycarbonate panel can divert rainfall away from the bed. Not a permanent solution but useful during known flood events.

    Raised Bed vs. Container — The Key Difference¶

    "When you fill a tall, confined bed with several hundred kilograms of soil, the bottom layers will compress under the weight, squeezing soil particles closer together, reducing pore spaces. Heavily compacted soil restricts drainage by causing water to become trapped in a waterlogged layer at the base." — Deep Green Permaculture (citing multiple extension sources)

    Your bed is more similar to a container than an in-ground bed: - No connection to ground soil means no downward percolation beyond the bed floor - The only exit for water is through walls (weep holes) or installed drains - Compression matters — as soil settles over years, base pore structure shrinks - Annual compost topdressing and no compaction (no walking in bed) preserves drainage
